In this profound verse, Sanjaya narrates to King Dhritarashtra that Lord Krishna, having spoken to Arjuna about His divine nature, now reveals His universal form (Vishwarupa). This moment marks a pivotal transition in the Bhagavad Gita, where Krishna moves beyond words to actual demonstration of His supreme cosmic nature. Sanjaya specifically addresses Dhritarashtra as "O King," creating a formal narrative frame for this extraordinary revelation that's about to unfold.
Sanjaya describes Krishna as "mahā-yogeśvaro hariḥ" - the Supreme Lord of all mystic power. This title is significant as it emphasizes Krishna's position not just as a teacher or friend to Arjuna, but as the master controller of all mystical powers in the universe. Unlike ordinary yogis who might perform minor miracles or even illusions, Krishna possesses the supreme ability to reveal the entire cosmic manifestation. This revelation isn't a mere display of power but a profound grace extended to Arjuna, who had requested to see this universal form.
The verse uses the term "paramaṁ rūpam aiśvaram" to describe what Krishna shows Arjuna - the supreme divine form that contains all of existence. This cosmic vision is something beyond ordinary human perception, requiring divine sight that Krishna specifically grants to Arjuna. The universal form would soon be described as containing countless mouths, eyes, ornaments, weapons, and divine features - a form so vast that it holds the entire cosmos within it, with a brilliance comparable to thousands of suns rising simultaneously.
Interestingly, this vision is simultaneously witnessed by Sanjaya himself, who has been blessed with divine sight by the sage Vyasa. This creates a narrative layer where Sanjaya serves as both witness and narrator, describing to the blind King Dhritarashtra what he himself is seeing. There's a poignant irony here - Dhritarashtra had previously declined the divine vision offered by Vyasa, thus missing not only this cosmic revelation but also the opportunity to have seen his own sons throughout his life. His refusal represents a deeper spiritual blindness beyond his physical condition.
This verse marks the beginning of a transformative experience for Arjuna. Until this moment, he had related to Krishna primarily as his charioteer, cousin, and friend. The revelation of the universal form would completely alter this perception, showing Arjuna that his companion is actually the Supreme Lord who contains and controls all of existence. This revelation would inspire awe, fear, and eventually deeper devotion in Arjuna, fundamentally changing the nature of their relationship.
The revelation of the universal form serves multiple purposes in the Gita's narrative. It provides irrefutable proof of Krishna's divine nature, confirms His teachings as supreme truth, and demonstrates that He is not merely a philosophical teacher but the actual embodiment of all that He has been describing. This cosmic vision also prepares Arjuna to receive Krishna's final instructions with complete faith and surrender, having witnessed firsthand the Lord's supreme position.
This verse reminds us of the importance of saying "yes" to spiritual opportunities in our lives. Just as Dhritarashtra missed experiencing this divine vision through his refusal, we too might close ourselves off from profound spiritual experiences when we approach life with negation rather than acceptance. The verse suggests that an attitude of openness, acceptance, and affirmation creates the conditions for experiencing deeper spiritual truths, while resistance and negation leave us in darkness, missing the extraordinary revelations that might otherwise transform our understanding.
