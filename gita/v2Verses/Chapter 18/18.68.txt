Verse 18.68 of the Bhagavad Gita represents a profound declaration by Lord Krishna about the sacred act of sharing divine knowledge. In this verse, Krishna states: "Amongst My devotees, those who teach this most confidential knowledge perform the greatest act of love. They will come to Me, without doubt." This verse appears at the conclusion of the Gita, where Krishna emphasizes not just the importance of receiving spiritual knowledge, but also the significance of transmitting it to others who are receptive.
The Sanskrit text "ya imam paramam guhyam madbhakteshvabhidhaasyati" refers specifically to those who explain this supreme secret to Krishna's devotees. Krishna isn't speaking about casual conversations or intellectual debates, but rather the sincere sharing of the Gita's wisdom with those who have some inclination toward devotional service. The term "paramam guhyam" (supreme secret) indicates that this knowledge is precious and profound – not meant to be distributed carelessly, but shared with those who can genuinely appreciate its value.
Krishna continues by saying that those who share this knowledge must do so with "bhaktim mayi paraam kritvaa" – having performed supreme devotion to Him. This suggests that effective teaching of the Gita's message isn't merely an intellectual exercise but requires the teacher to be engaged in devotional service themselves. The instruction must come from lived experience and genuine devotion, not from theoretical understanding alone. When teaching is performed as an act of devotion, it transforms into a form of worship itself.
The result of such sharing is explicitly stated: "maamevaishyatyasamshayaha" – they will certainly come to Krishna without doubt. This is an extraordinary guarantee from the Lord himself. Krishna doesn't say they might reach Him or that it's possible – He declares it with absolute certainty. This demonstrates how highly Krishna values the act of sharing spiritual knowledge with others. The teacher first attains "parama bhakti" (supreme devotion) and then reaches the Lord Himself.
What makes this verse particularly touching is how it reveals Krishna's intimate feelings toward those who share His message. It isn't merely transactional – Krishna doesn't just promise a reward for service rendered. Instead, He expresses a deep personal connection with those who teach the Gita. According to the commentaries, no one performs actions more pleasing to Krishna than the person who sincerely shares this knowledge with others. This indicates that teaching isn't just about benefiting others; it creates an intimate bond between the teacher and Krishna Himself.
The verse emphasizes that teaching should be done in the proper spirit – not for fame, name, or honor, but as service to the Lord. This aligns with the broader theme of the Gita, which consistently emphasizes selfless action performed as an offering to the divine. When instruction is given with this attitude, it becomes one of the highest forms of devotional service.
In essence, verse 18.68 reveals that sharing spiritual knowledge is not merely an obligation but a privilege that brings one closer to Krishna. It's a beautiful reminder that spiritual wisdom increases when shared with others, and that helping others on their devotional journey is itself a profound act of devotion. This verse offers encouragement to those who might feel hesitant about sharing their understanding of the Gita – Krishna assures them that sincere efforts to help others connect with divine wisdom make them especially dear to Him. By explaining the supreme secret of devotion to receptive hearts, one not only helps others but also deepens their own relationship with Krishna, ultimately ensuring their return to Him.
