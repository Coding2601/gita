In this verse of the Bhagavad Gita, Krishna makes a profound declaration about the nature of existence. He tells Arjuna that never was there a time when He did not exist, nor Arjuna, nor all the assembled kings, nor in the future shall any of them cease to exist. This statement is more than just a consolation in the face of imminent war—it is a revelation about the eternal reality underlying all beings.
Krishna’s words here fundamentally address the fear and confusion Arjuna faces as he grapples with the possibility of losing his loved ones in battle. By asserting the eternality of every soul present on the battlefield, Krishna expands Arjuna’s perception beyond the immediate reality of birth and death. He invites Arjuna to consider existence not just in terms of fleeting bodies, but in terms of the unchanging, deathless soul within each person.
This verse is striking for its assertion of plural individuality. Krishna explicitly mentions Himself, Arjuna, and all the kings assembled on the battlefield as eternally distinct individuals—there was never a time when they did not exist, and there will never be a time when they cease to exist. In doing so, Krishna dismisses the idea that the soul ultimately dissolves or merges into a vague oneness after liberation. Individual identity, according to Krishna, is not an illusion that dissolves with enlightenment or death but is an eternal feature of our true selves.
The precise Sanskrit phrase “na tv evāhaṁ jātu nāsaṁ na tvaṁ neme janādhipāḥ na caiva na bhaviṣyāmaḥ sarve vayam ataḥ param” reflects this continuity of being. Each pronoun—“I,” “you,” “these kings”—is emphatically stated, underscoring that everyone retains their unique identity eternally. This continuity is not affected by the death or dissolution of the body. The implication is that life is not a singular arc from birth to death, but an ongoing journey of an indestructible spirit.
Krishna also distinguishes all three basic realities—God, the soul, and the material energy—as separate, eternal entities. There is no suggestion that the soul, upon liberation, loses its nature or identity to merge with Krishna or the Absolute; rather, individuality persists through all circumstances, past, present, and future. This perspective counters both materialistic and certain monistic philosophies by affirming the perennial identity of the soul.
For Arjuna, who fears the end of relationships and the loss of loved ones, Krishna’s message transforms the entire meaning of life and mortality. The battlefield, filled with imminent danger and loss, becomes a stage where the eternal drama of the soul unfolds. By lifting the veil on the soul’s unending nature, Krishna provides a point of view that lessens the agony of separation and the anxiety surrounding death.
This teaching can feel both comforting and bewildering. On one hand, it reassures that neither we nor our loved ones truly cease to exist. On the other hand, it invites a rethinking of what it means to be alive and to relate to others. The verse gently nudges us to look beyond the mere physical aspects of existence and glimpse the constancy of the self that experiences life through many bodies and relationships, but never ends.
In this moment of the dialogue, Krishna is not offering a philosophical abstraction but a living truth. By affirming the shared eternity of Himself, Arjuna, and all others, Krishna dissolves the shadows cast by fear and loss. The soul’s journey, He suggests, continues uninterrupted, forever retaining its individuality, even as the external stories and identities shift through the cycles of time. Understanding this eternal dimension of being is the foundation upon which deeper wisdom and peace can be cultivated.
