In this profound verse (3.42) of the Bhagavad Gita, Lord Krishna reveals to Arjuna the hierarchical structure of our existence, presenting a key to mastering the self. Krishna establishes that the senses are superior to gross matter, the mind is superior to the senses, the intellect is superior to the mind, and the soul transcends even the intellect.
This verse follows Krishna's discussion on controlling lust, explaining that understanding this hierarchy is essential for spiritual advancement. Just as our hands can manipulate clay to form objects, our senses have power over physical matter. However, the mind holds authority over these senses - when we decide not to move a limb, it remains still despite external stimuli. The intellect, with its discriminative faculty, stands above the mind, allowing us to reject even those things we might crave if we determine they're harmful. At the summit of this hierarchy resides the soul (atman), which animates everything below it through consciousness.
Krishna's teaching can be visualized through the metaphor of a chariot: the physical body is the chariot itself, the five senses (sight, hearing, touch, taste, and smell) are the horses, sense objects are like grass attracting the horses, the mind forms the reins, intelligence serves as the charioteer, and the soul is the passenger. This elegant metaphor illustrates how each element relates to the others in our journey through life.
When properly aligned, this system functions harmoniously – the soul guides the intelligence, which directs the mind, which controls the senses, which interact appropriately with the material world. However, when this alignment breaks down, typically because the passenger (soul) hasn't established clear direction, the horses (senses) take control and pull the chariot wherever their immediate desires lead, often toward fleeting pleasures that ultimately bring suffering.
The verse underscores why spiritual traditions emphasize regulated lifestyle and disciplined practices. By systematically controlling sensory input through practices like fasting, controlling media consumption, or selective socializing, practitioners create space for higher faculties to assert their influence. This doesn't mean rejecting the world entirely, but rather engaging with it consciously and purposefully under the guidance of higher wisdom.
This hierarchy also illuminates different approaches to yoga. Those primarily identified with the physical realm naturally gravitate toward Karma Yoga (the yoga of action). Those operating predominantly at the mental level find Jnana Yoga (the yoga of knowledge) appealing. Those whose intelligence is highly developed often practice Dhyana Yoga (meditation). And those who recognize their spiritual identity as soul engage in Bhakti Yoga (devotional service), which contrary to some misconceptions, represents the most refined spiritual practice, occurring at the subtlest level of existence.
Ultimately, Krishna reveals this knowledge to help Arjuna understand how to transcend the pull of material desires and attachments. By recognizing the proper relationship between body, senses, mind, intellect, and soul, we gain the tools to establish control over our lives and align our actions with higher purpose. Rather than being dragged about by sensory urges like an uncontrolled chariot, we learn to direct our journey consciously, moving progressively toward spiritual realization and lasting fulfillment.
